# Rectal Neuroendocrine Tumor (Rectal NET) — Evidence Dossier

**Purpose.** Clinical decision-support evidence base for rectal neuroendocrine neoplasms, with an emphasis on cross-cancer translatable therapies and tissue-agnostic precedents. Every non-trivial claim is tied to a real PubMed PMID, DOI, or ClinicalTrials.gov NCT number. Items that could not be verified are marked **UNVERIFIED**.

**Scope note on terminology.** "Rectal NET" (well-differentiated neuroendocrine tumor) and "rectal/GI NEC" (poorly-differentiated neuroendocrine carcinoma) are biologically and therapeutically distinct diseases. This dossier keeps them separate because their molecular drivers and treatments diverge sharply. Where rectal-specific data are thin, GI/gastroenteropancreatic (GEP) data are used as the closest evidence and labeled as such.

Sources: PubMed (attribution required; DOIs linked inline), ClinicalTrials.gov API v2, FDA/NCI approval announcements, and Consensus. Compiled July 2026.

## 1. Disease Overview

**What it is / cell of origin.** Rectal NETs are epithelial neoplasms arising from the diffuse neuroendocrine cell system of the hindgut (historically "rectal carcinoids"). They are among the most frequent gastrointestinal neuroendocrine neoplasms and are usually detected incidentally at colonoscopy, most often during colorectal cancer screening (Cope & Srirajaskanthan, *Curr Oncol Rep* 2022, PMID 35084662, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11912-021-01172-1); Gallo et al., *World J Gastroenterol* 2022, PMID 35431507, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.3748/wjg.v28.i11.1123)).

**Classification (WHO / IARC framework).** The unifying principle, established by the IARC/WHO expert consensus (Rindi et al., *Mod Pathol* 2018, PMID 30140036, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41379-018-0110-y)) and carried into the 2019 WHO Classification of Digestive System Tumours (5th ed.; summarized by Quezada-Marín/Nagtegaal/Klimstra et al., *Histopathology* 2020, PMID 32320495, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/his.14120)), is a dichotomy between:

1. **Well-differentiated neuroendocrine tumors (NET)** — graded G1, G2, or G3 by proliferation.
2. **Poorly-differentiated neuroendocrine carcinomas (NEC)** — high-grade by definition (small-cell or large-cell type).

The key conceptual advance is that a well-differentiated **NET G3** (high proliferation but retained differentiation) is a different disease from a **NEC** (poorly differentiated), even though both are high-grade. This distinction is supported by genetic, epidemiologic, histologic, and prognostic differences (Rindi 2018, PMID 30140036, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41379-018-0110-y)).

**Grading (GEP sites).** Grade is assigned by mitotic count per 2 mm² and/or Ki-67 labeling index, with necrosis considered: G1 = Ki-67 <3% and <2 mitoses/2mm²; G2 = Ki-67 3–20% or 2–20 mitoses; G3 = Ki-67 >20% or >20 mitoses. NEC is high-grade by definition (Rindi et al. 2018, PMID 30140036, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41379-018-0110-y); WHO digestive tumours summary, PMID 32320495, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/his.14120)).

**Epidemiology / incidence.** Rectal NETs are the most common hindgut NET yet remain rare in absolute terms. They account for roughly 1–2% of all rectal tumors, are usually diagnosed at 50–60 years of age, and most are small (<10 mm) at detection (Cope & Srirajaskanthan 2022, PMID 35084662, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11912-021-01172-1)). Incidence is rising: US SEER data show the age-adjusted incidence of all NETs increased ~6.4-fold from 1973 (1.09/100,000) to 2012 (6.98/100,000), across all sites, stages, and grades (Dasari et al., *JAMA Oncol* 2017, PMID 28448665, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamaoncol.2017.0589)). Rectal NETs specifically show one of the largest incidence increases among GI sites, and there is marked ethnic variation with the highest rates reported in people of Asian ancestry (up to ~10-fold higher than white populations in some series) and elevated rates in Black and Hispanic populations (Cope & Srirajaskanthan 2022, PMID 35084662, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11912-021-01172-1)).

**Presentation.** Most rectal NETs are asymptomatic and found incidentally as small submucosal yellowish nodules at endoscopy; larger lesions may cause bleeding, pain, or change in bowel habit. Unlike some midgut NETs, rectal NETs rarely cause carcinoid syndrome (Gallo et al. 2022, PMID 35431507, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.3748/wjg.v28.i11.1123)).

**Prognosis by grade and stage.**
- Small, low-grade, localized rectal NETs (<10 mm, G1) have an excellent prognosis and are usually cured by local/endoscopic resection (Gallo et al. 2022, PMID 35431507, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.3748/wjg.v28.i11.1123)).
- Risk of lymph node and distant metastasis rises with size (≥10 mm), lymphovascular invasion, and muscularis propria involvement; rectal NETs with nodal metastasis behave far more aggressively (Fujimoto et al., *Langenbecks Arch Surg* 2009, PMID 19048280, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00423-008-0438-8); Gallo et al. 2022, PMID 35431507, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.3748/wjg.v28.i11.1123)).
- Across NETs generally, survival is strongly stage-, grade-, and site-dependent and has improved over time with better therapy (Dasari et al. 2017, PMID 28448665, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamaoncol.2017.0589)).
- **NEC** carries a poor prognosis. In advanced GI-NEC (WHO G3), median overall survival is ~11 months with palliative chemotherapy and ~1 month with best supportive care alone; a Ki-67 threshold near 55% separates a more chemo-responsive but shorter-lived high group from a less chemo-responsive but longer-lived lower group (Sorbye et al., NORDIC NEC study, *Ann Oncol* 2013, PMID 22967994, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1093/annonc/mds276)).

**Why the NET/NEC split matters clinically.** Well-differentiated NETs are treated with somatostatin analogs, targeted agents (everolimus, sunitinib for pancreatic), and peptide receptor radionuclide therapy (PRRT), leveraging retained somatostatin-receptor biology. NECs are treated like small-cell lung cancer with platinum-etoposide chemotherapy. Confusing the two leads to under- or over-treatment (Rindi 2018, PMID 30140036, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41379-018-0110-y); Sorbye 2013, PMID 22967994, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1093/annonc/mds276)).

## 2. Molecular Drivers

Drivers segregate cleanly by differentiation. The single richest source for high-grade GEP disease (including rectal-specific data) is a 181-patient targeted-sequencing series (Venizelos et al., *Endocr Relat Cancer* 2021/2022, PMID 34647903, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1530/ERC-21-0152)).

### Well-differentiated NET
- **Pancreatic NET (contrast entity).** MEN1 inactivation ~44%, DAXX/ATRX ~43% combined, and mTOR-pathway genes (PTEN/TSC2/PIK3CA) ~14%; MEN1 and DAXX/ATRX associate with better prognosis, and mTOR-pathway mutations were proposed to stratify for mTOR inhibitors (Jiao et al., *Science* 2011, PMID 21252315, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1200609)).
- **Well-differentiated NET G3 (GEP).** A distinct profile from NEC: MEN1 21%, ATRX 17%, and DAXX/SETD2/TP53 each ~14%, with essentially no BRAF (Venizelos 2021, PMID 34647903, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1530/ERC-21-0152)).
- **Small-intestinal NET (for contrast).** Genomically quiet; CDKN1B is the only recurrent mutation and at low frequency (~8%), with frequent chromosome 18 loss; microsatellite-stable, low tumor mutational burden (Stålberg et al., *J Intern Med* 2016, PMID 27306880, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/joim.12526)).
- **SSTR2 (somatostatin receptor 2).** Characteristically retained/high in well-differentiated NET and lost with dedifferentiation; low SSTR2 independently predicts shorter survival in GEP-NET (Pinato et al., *Br J Cancer* 2013, PMID 24231952, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/bjc.2013.682)). This retained SSTR2 biology underpins somatostatin-analog therapy, PRRT, and 68Ga-DOTATATE imaging.

### Poorly-differentiated NEC (high-grade)
- **TP53 + RB1.** TP53 is the dominant NEC event (~64%). RB1 is inactivated more often by copy-number loss (~34%) than by point mutation (~14%); combined TP53/RB1 loss was functionally validated as a driver in organoid models. Notably, GEP-NEC is molecularly **less "SCLC-like" than assumed** (Venizelos 2021, PMID 34647903, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1530/ERC-21-0152); Kawasaki et al., *Cell* 2020, PMID 33159857, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2020.10.023); Girardi et al., *Cancer Treat Rev* 2017, PMID 28456055, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ctrv.2017.04.002)).
- **KRAS** ~22% in GEP-NEC (Venizelos 2021, PMID 34647903, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1530/ERC-21-0152)).
- **BRAF** ~20% overall in GEP-NEC, strongly colorectal-enriched (colon ~49%), and tightly linked to MSI; less frequent in rectal than colonic NEC (Venizelos 2021, PMID 34647903, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1530/ERC-21-0152); Sahnane et al., *Endocr Relat Cancer* 2015, PMID 25465415, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1530/ERC-14-0410)).
- **APC / Wnt** ~28% in GEP-NEC, reflecting an adenocarcinoma-like genotype in many colorectal NECs (Venizelos 2021, PMID 34647903, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1530/ERC-21-0152)).
- **Rectal-specific NEC (n=36).** TP53 61%, APC 53%, KRAS 25%, BRAF 25%, with **FBXW7 enrichment (~25%)** as the standout rectal feature and MYC amplification ~33% (Venizelos 2021, PMID 34647903, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1530/ERC-21-0152)).
- **MSI-H / dMMR.** Present in ~5–12% of GI/colorectal NEC; mostly gastric and intestinal, usually driven by MLH1 promoter methylation with MLH1 protein loss, associated with BRAF mutation and better prognosis (Sahnane et al. 2015: 12.4%, PMID 25465415, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1530/ERC-14-0410); Venizelos 2021: 5.3% overall, PMID 34647903, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1530/ERC-21-0152); Girardi 2017 review: ~10% in gastric/colorectal NEC, PMID 28456055, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ctrv.2017.04.002)).
- **High TMB.** GEP-NEC median TMB is low (~5.1/Mb) but the MSI-H subset reaches the high-TMB range (up to ~69/Mb); i.e., high TMB in GEP-NEC essentially overlaps the MSI-H/MLH1-loss subset (Venizelos 2021, PMID 34647903, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1530/ERC-21-0152)).
- **DLL3.** Surface Notch-ligand DLL3 is expressed in a large fraction of GEP-NEC and is a strong marker of poor differentiation: DLL3 positivity ~54.8% in GEP-NEC vs 0% in NET G1/G2 and 15.8% in NET G3, correlating with small-cell morphology and ASCL1 (Yin et al., *Sci Rep* 2025, PMID 39865119, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-025-86237-y)). DLL3 is functionally relevant in GI-NEC (knockdown induces apoptosis; Matsuo et al., *Cancer Sci* 2019, PMID 31369178, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/cas.14157)).
- **NTRK fusions.** Very rare in neuroendocrine neoplasms (well under 1%), occasionally actionable; NTRK fusions across cancers are enriched in rare morphologies and MSI colorectal tumors (O'Haire et al., *Sci Rep* 2023, PMID 36914665, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-023-31055-3)).
- **ctDNA driver spectrum** in GEP/CUP-NEC mirrors tissue (TP53 dominant; KRAS/BRAF/RB1 recurrent), and KRAS/BRAF mutations were associated with shorter platinum-etoposide PFS in a small pilot (Gerard et al., *Neuroendocrinology* 2020, PMID 33099543, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1159/000512502); **flag: small pilot**).

**Bottom line.** Well-differentiated NET = MEN1 / DAXX-ATRX / mTOR (pancreatic), quiet MSS genome (small intestine), retained SSTR2. Poorly-differentiated NEC = TP53 + RB1, with KRAS/BRAF/APC giving colorectal NEC an adenocarcinoma-like genotype, a targetable MSI-H/high-TMB subset, and frequent DLL3 surface expression.

## 3. Standard of Care (by grade / stage)

### Localized rectal NET (well-differentiated)
Size-stratified per ENETS guidance (Gallo et al. 2022, PMID 35431507, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.3748/wjg.v28.i11.1123); de Mestier et al., *Best Pract Res Clin Endocrinol Metab* 2019, PMID 31326374, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.beem.2019.101293)):
- **<10 mm, well-differentiated, no high-risk features →** endoscopic resection (low risk of nodal/distant spread).
- **10–20 mm →** intermediate metastatic risk; individualized endoscopic vs surgical resection with staging.
- **≥20 mm, muscularis propria invasion, or nodal disease →** surgical resection.
Risk factors for nodal metastasis (mandating more aggressive management) include size ≥10 mm and lymphovascular invasion (Fujimoto et al. 2009, PMID 19048280, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00423-008-0438-8)).

### Advanced / metastatic well-differentiated NET
- **Somatostatin analogs (SSAs).** Lanreotide prolonged PFS vs placebo in metastatic enteropancreatic NET (CLARINET; Caplin et al., *N Engl J Med* 2014, PMID 25014687, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1316158)). Octreotide LAR is the class comparator and standard first-line antiproliferative/antisecretory therapy.
- **PRRT (Lu-177 DOTATATE).** In advanced, progressive, SSTR-positive midgut NET, 177Lu-DOTATATE markedly improved PFS vs high-dose octreotide (NETTER-1; Strosberg et al., *N Engl J Med* 2017, PMID 28076709, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1607427)). Applies to SSTR-positive GI NETs generally.
- **Everolimus (mTOR inhibitor).** RADIANT-4 showed everolimus improved PFS (11.0 vs 3.9 months; HR 0.48) in advanced, progressive, non-functional lung/GI NET (Yao et al., *Lancet* 2016, PMID 26703889, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(15)00817-X)).
- **Sunitinib (VEGFR TKI).** Approved for advanced well-differentiated **pancreatic** NET (PFS 11.4 vs 5.5 months; HR 0.42); relevant to rectal NET only by pathway analogy, not by label (Raymond et al., *N Engl J Med* 2011, PMID 21306237, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1003825)).

### Neuroendocrine carcinoma (NEC, high-grade)
- **Platinum-etoposide chemotherapy** is first-line, borrowed directly from small-cell lung cancer. Median OS ~11 months with palliative chemo; a Ki-67 cutoff near 55% predicts platinum responsiveness (higher Ki-67 = more responsive but shorter survival) (Sorbye et al., NORDIC NEC, *Ann Oncol* 2013, PMID 22967994, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1093/annonc/mds276)).
- Second-line options are limited and often extrapolated from SCLC; this is the major unmet need that motivates Section 5.

## 5. Cross-Cancer Translatable Therapies (PRIORITY SECTION)

**Evidence tiers.**
- **Tier 1** = FDA tissue-agnostic approval applicable if the biomarker is present (e.g., MSI-H → pembrolizumab).
- **Tier 2** = approved in another tumor type with the SAME target present in NET/NEC, OR a positive basket/prospective signal specifically in NET/NEC.
- **Tier 3** = mechanistic/preclinical rationale only, no clinical NET/NEC data.

### Tissue-agnostic (Tier 1) — highest-confidence translations
1. **Pembrolizumab for MSI-H/dMMR (Tier 1).** FDA's FIRST tissue-agnostic approval (May 23, 2017) covers MSI-H/dMMR unresectable/metastatic solid tumors — directly applicable to the ~5–12% of GI/colorectal NEC that are MSI-H (Sahnane PMID 25465415, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1530/ERC-14-0410)). Registrational basket evidence: KEYNOTE-158 noncolorectal MSI-H/dMMR cohort, ORR 34.3% across 27 tumor types including neuroendocrine (Marabelle et al., *J Clin Oncol* 2019, PMID 31682550, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1200/JCO.19.02105); updated Maio et al., *Ann Oncol* 2022, PMID 35680043, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.annonc.2022.05.519)).
2. **Pembrolizumab for TMB-high ≥10 mut/Mb (Tier 1).** FDA tissue-agnostic approval (Jun 16, 2020). KEYNOTE-158 prospectively enrolled a neuroendocrine cohort; tTMB-high ORR 29% vs 6% in non-TMB-high (Marabelle et al., *Lancet Oncol* 2020, PMID 32919526, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1470-2045(20)30445-9)). Applies to the high-TMB (largely MSI-H) NEC subset.
3. **Dostarlimab for dMMR solid tumors (Tier 1).** FDA tissue-agnostic accelerated approval (Aug 2021) for dMMR recurrent/advanced solid tumors; GARNET dMMR ORR 44.0% (André et al., *JAMA Netw Open* 2023, PMID 37917058, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2023.41165)). Alternative anti-PD-1 for the dMMR NEC subset.
4. **Larotrectinib for NTRK fusions (Tier 1).** FDA tissue-agnostic approval; pooled ORR 79% across TRK fusion solid tumors (Hong et al., *Lancet Oncol* 2020, PMID 32105622, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1470-2045(19)30856-3)). Applicable only to the very rare NTRK-fusion NEN (O'Haire PMID 36914665, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-023-31055-3)).
5. **Entrectinib for NTRK fusions (Tier 1).** FDA tissue-agnostic approval (Aug 2019); integrated ORR 57% (Doebele/Demetri et al., *Lancet Oncol* 2020, PMID 31838007, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1470-2045(19)30691-6)). The entrectinib basket (NCT02568267) explicitly includes neuroendocrine tumors.
6. **Dabrafenib + trametinib for BRAF V600E (Tier 1).** FDA tissue-agnostic approval (Jun 2022); ROAR basket ORR ~51% in biliary cohort (Subbiah et al., *Lancet Oncol* 2020, PMID 32818466, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1470-2045(20)30321-1)). Directly relevant to BRAF V600E colorectal/rectal NEC (~25% rectal; Venizelos PMID 34647903, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1530/ERC-21-0152)).
7. **Selpercatinib for RET fusions (Tier 1).** FDA tissue-agnostic approval (Sep 2022); LIBRETTO-001 tumor-agnostic ORR 43.9% (Subbiah/Drilon et al., *Lancet Oncol* 2022, PMID 36108661, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1470-2045(22)00541-1)). Applicable only if a (rare) RET fusion is documented in a rectal NEN — **biomarker-gated, RET prevalence in rectal NEN UNVERIFIED**.
8. **Trastuzumab deruxtecan for HER2 IHC 3+ (Tier 1).** FDA tissue-agnostic accelerated approval (Apr 2024); DESTINY-PanTumor02 IHC 3+ ORR 61.3% (Meric-Bernstam et al., *J Clin Oncol* 2024, PMID 37870536, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1200/JCO.23.02005)). Applicable only if HER2 IHC 3+ is documented — **HER2 IHC 3+ prevalence in rectal NEN UNVERIFIED; include only as biomarker-gated option.**

### Same-target, other-tumor (Tier 2)
9. **Tarlatamab (DLL3 × CD3 bispecific T-cell engager) (Tier 2).** FDA-approved 2024 for previously treated SCLC based on DeLLphi-301 (ORR 40%; Ahn et al., *N Engl J Med* 2023, PMID 37861218, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2307980)). DLL3 is expressed in ~55% of GEP-NEC (Yin PMID 39865119, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-025-86237-y)), providing a strong same-target rationale; tarlatamab is now in dedicated extrapulmonary/GEP-NEC trials (NCT06893783 phase 2; NCT06937905 phase 3). Rises toward Tier 2 by virtue of active prospective NEC trials and shared target.
10. **Ipilimumab + nivolumab (dual checkpoint blockade) (Tier 2).** In the DART SWOG 1609 rare-tumor basket, the non-pancreatic neuroendocrine cohort had an overall ORR 25%, but high-grade NEC ORR was 44% (8/18) vs 0% in low/intermediate-grade NET (Patel et al., *Clin Cancer Res* 2020, PMID 31969335, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1158/1078-0432.CCR-19-3356)). Positive prospective NET/NEC basket signal.
11. **Platinum-etoposide (Tier 2, established SoC borrowed from SCLC).** The original and most successful cross-cancer translation: SCLC chemotherapy is standard for NEC (Sorbye et al. 2013, PMID 22967994, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1093/annonc/mds276)).

### Mechanistic only (Tier 3)
12. **DLL3-directed ADCs / CAR-T / other T-cell engagers (Tier 3 for rectal NEC).** DLL3-targeted modalities beyond tarlatamab are in early development across DLL3+ NEN including GEP-NEC (e.g., peluntamig/PT217, obrixtamig/BI 764532 — see Section 6); rationale is strong (Yao et al. review, *Oncologist* 2022, PMID 35983951, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1093/oncolo/oyac161)) but rectal-NEC clinical data are not yet mature. Tier 3 until trial readouts.

## 7. Tissue-Agnostic Precedent Primer

The intellectual backbone of biomarker-driven, site-independent oncology. Each landmark below is FDA-approved for use in any solid tumor bearing the biomarker.

1. **Pembrolizumab, MSI-H/dMMR — May 2017.** The FIRST tissue-agnostic oncology approval; based on KEYNOTE-016/012/028/158/164 pooled data (Marabelle et al., *J Clin Oncol* 2019, PMID 31682550, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1200/JCO.19.02105)).
2. **Larotrectinib, NTRK fusion — 2018.** Pooled ORR 79% (Hong et al., *Lancet Oncol* 2020, PMID 32105622, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1470-2045(19)30856-3)).
3. **Entrectinib, NTRK fusion — Aug 2019.** Integrated ORR 57% (Doebele/Demetri et al., *Lancet Oncol* 2020, PMID 31838007, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1470-2045(19)30691-6)).
4. **Pembrolizumab, TMB-high ≥10 mut/Mb — Jun 2020.** KEYNOTE-158 biomarker analysis (Marabelle et al., *Lancet Oncol* 2020, PMID 32919526, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1470-2045(20)30445-9)).
5. **Dostarlimab, dMMR — 2021.** GARNET dMMR ORR 44% (André et al., *JAMA Netw Open* 2023, PMID 37917058, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2023.41165)).
6. **Dabrafenib + trametinib, BRAF V600E — Jun 2022.** ROAR basket (Subbiah et al., *Lancet Oncol* 2020, PMID 32818466, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1470-2045(20)30321-1)).
7. **Selpercatinib, RET fusion — Sep 2022.** LIBRETTO-001 tumor-agnostic cohort, ORR 43.9% (Subbiah/Drilon et al., *Lancet Oncol* 2022, PMID 36108661, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1470-2045(22)00541-1)).
8. **Trastuzumab deruxtecan, HER2 IHC 3+ — Apr 2024.** First tumor-agnostic HER2-directed ADC; DESTINY-PanTumor02 IHC 3+ ORR 61.3% (Meric-Bernstam et al., *J Clin Oncol* 2024, PMID 37870536, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1200/JCO.23.02005)).
